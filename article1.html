How to Reduce Food Waste & Cook Sustainably

Growing up, I loved cooking, so I regularly cooked my own dinners, breakfasts, and packed my own lunch. It was easy, the food was always in the fridge because my parents were the ones grocery shopping!

When I came to college, I soon realized how difficult it is to shop and cook for one person. I found ease in overspending on groceries and every recipe I made lasted a week! By day three I’d be so tired of whatever I made, I’d end up throwing the rest away. If I didn’t throw it away it would just end up sitting there until I finally decided it was time to let it go. I felt really guilty about wasting all that food so I began to adjust my shopping and recipes to cater to one person. Here are some tips on how I managed to do that!

1. Find 3-4 recipes you eat regularly that you enjoy. For example, mine were avocado toast with eggs, stir fry, pesto pasta, and quesadillas. Here is what a normal list would look like:

Ingredients

☺ 1 bag of Bread

☺ 4 Avocados

☺ 1 pint of Cherry Tomatoes

☺ 1 carton Eggs

☺ 1 bag Rice

☺ 2 crowns of Broccoli

☺ 3 Peppers (1 red, 1 orange, 1 yellow)

☺ 1 bag Carrots

☺ 2 bags Mexican Cheese

☺ 1 can Corn

☺ 2 Jalapenos

☺ 1 Red Onion

☺ 1 Lime

☺ Tortillas

Each of these ingredients can be used in all of those recipes, for example broccoli and peppers could be used in pesto pasta and stir fry. The peppers could also be used in the quesadilla as well. This typically covers my breakfast and dinner, for lunch I would have peanut butter with grapes or a protein bar. Depending on how much you eat you can adjust this list, but finding commonalities in the recipes you like is very helpful when grocery shopping! Each week you can build upon what you have left over. Often, I would still have Mexican cheese and corn salsa, so the next week I would just buy more tortillas. Using this method, I could make a different dinner every night without breaking the bank.

2. Stop buying name brand. Buying name brand items is like spending extra for the look of the container that the food comes in, even though the difference could be cents, it really does add up. In addition to that, I also try not to buy any packaged vegetables. Vegetables that come in plastic tend to be more expensive and create more waste!
3. Another way to avoid breaking the bank is to get a membership card at your local grocery store. This is another example of where “it really adds up”, you may only be saving a few extra dollars each time, but if every week you save 5 dollars, by the end of the semester that’s 70 dollars. That’s 14 coffees from starbucks, 23 coffees from Dunkin, or more than a full tank of gas! My point is, it’s worth it to save where you can when it’s that easy. Make sure to look for the store brand, it typically looks simpler than the brand name and is usually right next to it.
4. Your freezer is your best friend! It’s easy to mess up, maybe it just so happened that you ate out a lot this week or just didn’t eat as much as you expected. A lot of veggies, fruits, and even bread can be frozen and eaten when you’re ready for them. I go through bread pretty slowly, so I usually store mine in the freezer. Worst case scenario it takes a little bit longer to cook! I also tend to buy frozen berries since berries don’t last very long. Throwing them in the microwave for a minute and adding some greek yogurt makes the perfect snack!
